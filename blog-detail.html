Why Your Back Hurts — and What You Can Do About It

A practical guide to understanding back pain, building confidence in movement and knowing when professional support can help.

By AlignAsya Physiotherapy Team · July 2026

Back pain can make ordinary activities feel unexpectedly difficult. The good news is that many people can improve their comfort and function through a combination of education, gradual movement and an individualized rehabilitation plan.

First, understand the bigger picture

Back pain can have many contributing factors, including changes in activity, muscle weakness, repetitive loading, stress, sleep and previous injuries. Pain does not always mean that something is seriously damaged. A thorough assessment helps identify patterns and guide the right next steps.

The goal is not simply to chase pain away. It is to help you understand your body, restore confidence in movement and return to the activities that matter to you.

Three principles that can help

1. Keep moving within your comfort

Long periods of complete rest are not ideal for many common episodes of back pain. Gentle walking, comfortable mobility and gradual activity can help maintain function. If a movement consistently worsens symptoms, modify it and seek professional guidance.

2. Build strength gradually

Strength is built through consistent, progressive loading. Your physiotherapist can help choose exercises appropriate to your current ability and progress them as your confidence improves.

3. Look beyond the painful area

Recovery is influenced by the whole person. Sleep, stress, work setup, activity levels and daily habits can all play a role. A good plan considers your lifestyle rather than focusing only on one body part.

When should you seek professional help?

Consider an assessment if pain is persistent, keeps returning, limits your daily activities or makes you hesitant to move. Seek urgent medical attention for severe symptoms such as new loss of bladder or bowel control, significant weakness, numbness around the saddle area, or back pain following major trauma.
